\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% =============================================================================

\paragraph{SOC turnover governs the climate feedback.}

Soils store approximately twice as much carbon as the atmosphere, and the
rate at which this carbon cycles through the soil system is a primary
determinant of the land carbon--climate feedback. Whether soils act as a
net source or sink under changing conditions depends not on stocks alone
but on turnover --- specifically, on the mean transit time ($\tau$) of
carbon through the soil, defined as the average time elapsed between a
carbon atom entering and leaving the system
\citep{Sierra2017,Sierra2018}. Even modest shifts in transit times
across large areas can alter the global carbon balance substantially.

\paragraph{The classical paradigm: chemistry and constants.}

Since the foundational models of the 1970s--1990s
% [CITE: Jenkinson, Parton/CENTURY, RothC lineage]
, soil organic carbon (SOC) decomposition has been represented as a
first-order process: the rate of loss from each pool is proportional to
its mass, scaled by functions of temperature and moisture. The rate
constant~$k$ --- and thus the transit time $\tau = 1/k$ --- is treated as
an intrinsic property of the pool, spatially invariant once the
environmental scalars are applied. This framework descends from the
chemical recalcitrance paradigm, in which substrate chemistry determines
decomposability, and the chemistry of soil organic matter is assumed
broadly similar across environments. Earth system models (ESMs) still
largely rely on this architecture.
% [CITE: Todd-Brown et al. 2013; Wieder et al. 2013]

\paragraph{The paradigm shift: persistence as an ecosystem property.}

Over the past two decades, this view has been increasingly challenged.
\citet{Schmidt2011} argued that SOC persistence is not a molecular
property but an ecosystem property, emerging from interactions between
organic matter, mineral surfaces, microbial communities, and the
physical structure of the soil matrix. Subsequent work has reinforced
this perspective: organo-mineral associations and reactive mineral
surfaces stabilise organic matter independently of its chemistry
% [CITE: Kleber et al. 2007, 2021; Lehmann \& Kleber 2015]
; microbial carbon use efficiency varies with community composition
% [CITE: Kallenbach et al. 2016; Cotrufo et al. MEMS]
; priming effects modulate the decomposition of existing stocks in
response to fresh inputs; and mycorrhizal pathways influence both the
quantity and chemical nature of belowground carbon transfers
% [CITE: Averill et al. 2014]
. The implication is that decomposition kinetics are not constant ---
they arise from a web of interacting ecological, edaphic, and
biological processes operating locally.

\paragraph{The zonality hypothesis.}

If transit times are emergent properties of local ecology, they should
exhibit spatial structure --- a ``zonality'' analogous to the classical
pedogenic zonality where soil types co-vary with climate, parent
material, organisms, topography, and time. Under this view, SOC transit
times would not be universal constants scaled by temperature and
moisture alone, but would carry a residual spatial signal reflecting
edaphic, biological, and land-use context. This residual represents the
variance that current ESMs do not capture.

\paragraph{The modelling dilemma.}

One could attempt to represent these interactions mechanistically, but
doing so introduces a different class of risk. Any mechanistic model
imposes a simplified structure on a complex, potentially nonlinear
system. Processes may be omitted not because data are lacking but
because they are not yet recognised, or because they operate through
interactions invisible at the chosen scale of simplification. The model
then risks encoding our current biases about which processes matter: a
detailed mechanistic representation that is wrong about the structure
of interactions may describe calibration data well --- SOC models are
already overparameterised relative to available observations --- yet
fail to extrapolate spatially and, more critically, temporally. Working
instead at the level of the emergent kinetic property --- the
observable, bulk transit time --- and exploring its spatial covariance
with environmental covariates may introduce less structural error when
extrapolating, precisely because it does not commit to a specific
internal process architecture that may be incomplete or wrong.
Introducing the zonality of SOC dynamics directly into models ---
parameterising transit times as spatially varying quantities that
reflect local ecological context --- could be a way to capture this
missing variance without the risks of mechanistic overspecification.

\paragraph{This study.}

As an exploratory step in that direction, we compile a global dataset of
soil profiles, calculate apparent mean transit times under steady-state
first-order assumptions \citep[sensu][]{Sierra2017,Sierra2018}, and ask:
how much of the spatial variation in transit times can be attributed to
processes beyond climate, and which process classes matter where? We use
a nested Random Forest framework to partition variance across four
groups --- climate, edaphic, land use, and biological --- where the
change in performance between the climate-only baseline (M1) and the
full model (M7) directly quantifies the ``missing zonality'' that
current ESMs do not represent. The steady-state assumption is a known
simplification; over a dataset of this size and geographic scope, errors
from non-equilibrium sites are expected to partially cancel. Accumulated
Local Effects analysis identifies the marginal response of transit times
to individual predictors, revealing the functional shape of the
relationships --- information that could inform how zonality might
eventually be parameterised. The resulting observation-constrained maps
provide a benchmark for identifying where and why ESMs systematically
diverge from data-driven estimates.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}
% =============================================================================

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Conceptual framework}
\label{sec:framework}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

We define the mean transit time ($\tau$) of SOC as the average time a
carbon atom spends in the soil from entry to exit
\citep{Sierra2017,Sierra2018}. Under steady-state assumptions (constant
SOC stock and input flux) and a single homogeneous pool, $\tau$ equals
the ratio of the SOC stock to the annual carbon input flux and is the
inverse of the apparent first-order decomposition rate constant~$k$:

\begin{equation}
  \tau \;=\; \frac{\text{SOC}_\text{stock}}{C_\text{input}}
  \;=\; \frac{1}{k}
  \label{eq:mtt}
\end{equation}

\noindent
We acknowledge that for multi-pool systems, $\tau$ as computed here
represents a bulk diagnostic that conflates pools with different
turnover rates \citep{Sierra2018}. Nevertheless, this metric is widely
used as a first-order characterisation of SOC dynamics and is directly
comparable to the transit time diagnostics computed from ESM output
\citep{Carvalhais2014,Sierra2017}.

To separate the contributions of different process classes to spatial
variation in $\tau$, we designed a nested Random Forest (RF) modelling
framework in which predictor variables are organised into four
mechanistically distinct groups: Climate, Edaphic, Land~Use, and
Biological (Table~\ref{tab:predictors}). Seven model configurations
(M1--M7) progressively combine these groups
(Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison}), so that the change in cross-validated
performance between successive models isolates the additional explanatory
power of each process class while holding the climate baseline constant.

A key design constraint is the exclusion of productivity-related
variables (net primary productivity, leaf area index) from the predictor
set. Because $\tau$ is calculated as SOC stock divided by an NPP-derived
input flux (Eq.~\ref{eq:mtt}), including productivity as a predictor
would introduce a circular dependency.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Soil data compilation}
\label{sec:data}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

We compiled soil profile data from the World Soil Information Service
\citep[WoSIS;][]{Batjes2024} point dataset
(\texttt{sol\_chem.pnts\_horizons}), which integrates observations from
multiple national and regional soil surveys worldwide. From the full
dataset of horizon-level records, we retained only mineral soils by
removing all records with organic carbon (OC)
$\geq$~120~g~kg$^{-1}$ (the conventional threshold separating mineral
from organic soil horizons). Records with missing OC were retained.

Horizon-level data were standardised to a 0--20~cm depth interval by
depth-weighted averaging. For each unique soil profile (identified by
site~ID), we computed the overlap of each recorded horizon with the
0--20~cm target interval and calculated a thickness-weighted mean for
seven soil variables: organic carbon, total nitrogen, pH~(H$_2$O), clay
content, silt content, sand content, and oven-dry bulk density.
Site-level metadata (coordinates, observation date, source database)
were carried forward as the most common value across horizons.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Bulk density gap-filling}
\label{sec:bd}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Bulk density (BD) is required to convert OC concentration to SOC stock
but is frequently unreported. We gap-filled missing BD values using a
three-tier approach. First, a Random Forest model
(\texttt{randomForest} R~package; 200~trees) was trained on the subset
of profiles with measured BD, using terrain metrics, K\"oppen zone, land
cover fractions, SoilGrids soil properties (excluding BD), MODIS NPP,
WRB functional group, and MODIS burn count as predictors. Ten-fold
cross-validation confirmed that the RF model outperformed the SoilGrids
BD product for sites with measured BD. Second, profiles lacking measured
BD but with complete RF predictors received RF-predicted values. Third,
remaining gaps were filled with SoilGrids BD as a fallback. Each profile
was flagged with its BD source (measured, RF, or SoilGrids).

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Mean transit time calculation}
\label{sec:mtt_calc}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

SOC stock for the 0--20~cm layer was calculated as:

\begin{equation}
  \text{SOC}_\text{stock}\;[\text{g\,C\,m}^{-2}]
  \;=\; \text{OC} \;\times\; \text{BD} \;\times\; 20 \;\times\; 10
  \label{eq:soc_stock}
\end{equation}

\noindent
where OC is in g~kg$^{-1}$, BD in g~cm$^{-3}$, depth is 20~cm, and the
factor~10 converts units.

Carbon input to soil was estimated as the belowground fraction of net
primary productivity (BNPP). We used MODIS MOD17A3 annual NPP
\citep[2001--2020;][]{Running2015} extracted at each profile location,
applying the MODIS scale factor
($\text{NPP}_\text{true} = \text{NPP}_\text{raw} / 10$;
g~C~m$^{-2}$~yr$^{-1}$). To maintain temporal consistency with the
equilibrium assumption, only NPP values from years preceding each
site's sampling date were used. Sites sampled before the MODIS era
(pre-2001) or with fewer than three pre-sampling NPP years were
excluded from the temporal statistics.

Belowground allocation fractions (BNPP/NPP) were assigned by dominant
land cover class following \citet{Mokany2006}, \citet{Malhi2011}, and
\citet{Litton2008}: tree cover 0.35, shrubland 0.45, grassland 0.55,
cropland 0.30, herbaceous wetland 0.50, mangroves 0.40, moss/lichen
0.60. Sites lacking land cover information received a global average of
0.40. Soil carbon input was then:

\begin{equation}
  C_\text{input}\;[\text{g\,C\,m}^{-2}\text{\,yr}^{-1}]
  \;=\; \text{NPP} \;\times\; f_\text{BNPP}
  \label{eq:c_input}
\end{equation}

\noindent
and $\tau$ was computed as the ratio of SOC stock to $C_\text{input}$
(Eq.~\ref{eq:mtt}). Quality control flags were assigned: values with
$\tau < 1$~year or $> 1000$~years, or with missing, negative, or
infinite values were excluded. The upper and lower 1\% tails of the
remaining distribution were removed prior to model fitting.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Predictor variables}
\label{sec:predictors}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Predictor variables were organised into four groups reflecting distinct
mechanisms influencing SOC turnover (Table~\ref{tab:predictors}).

\subsubsection{Climate}

Climate variables were derived from the ERA5-Land reanalysis monthly
means \citep[2001--2020;][]{MunozSabater2021}. From the original monthly
fields of 2-m air temperature (\texttt{t2m}), soil temperature at
layers~1 (0--7~cm; \texttt{stl1}) and~2 (7--28~cm; \texttt{stl2}),
volumetric soil water at layers~1 (\texttt{swvl1}) and~2
(\texttt{swvl2}), snow cover fraction (\texttt{snowc}), and potential
evaporation (\texttt{pev}), we calculated the following annual
statistics: mean 2-m temperature, temperature seasonality (monthly
standard deviation), depth-weighted mean soil temperature for 0--20~cm
(weights 0.35 for layer~1 and 0.65 for layer~2, proportional to the
overlap with the 0--20~cm interval), depth-weighted mean soil moisture
(same weights), mean snow cover fraction, and mean potential
evaporation. Climate zone was represented by the K\"oppen-Geiger
classification of \citet{Beck2023} at 30-class resolution (numeric
codes 1--30), based on the 1991--2020 climatological period at
${\sim}$1~km native resolution, resampled to 0.1\textdegree{} by modal
aggregation.

\subsubsection{Edaphic}

Soil properties were obtained from SoilGrids 250\,m v2.0
\citep{Poggio2021}, depth-weighted to 0--20~cm: clay content, sand
content, bulk density, coarse fragment volume, pH~(H$_2$O), cation
exchange capacity, and total nitrogen. All values were converted from
SoilGrids integer encoding to standard units using published scale
factors.

Terrain metrics were derived from the Copernicus GLO-30 Digital
Elevation Model \citep{CopernicusDEM2022}, aggregated to
0.1\textdegree{}: mean elevation, mean slope, northness and eastness
(sine and cosine of aspect), and terrain ruggedness (focal standard
deviation of elevation in a $3 \times 3$ cell window, $\sim$30~km).

Soil classification was based on the SoilGrids WRB Most Probable class
\citep[250\,m;][]{Poggio2021}, aggregated to 0.1\textdegree{} by mode
and reclassified into eight mechanistically defined functional groups:
sandy/coarse (Arenosols, Podzols, Regosols), temperate agricultural
(Luvisols, Cambisols, Phaeozems, Chernozems, Kastanozems,
Albeluvisols), high-clay tropical (Acrisols, Ferralsols, Lixisols,
Plinthosols, Nitisols, Alisols, Vertisols), arid/chemical (Calcisols,
Gypsisols, Solonchaks, Solonetz, Durisols), poorly drained (Gleysols,
Histosols, Fluvisols, Stagnosols, Planosols), volcanic (Andosols),
shallow/young (Leptosols, Umbrisols), and cold/permafrost (Cryosols).
These groups were encoded as integer codes for the RF models.

\subsubsection{Land use and disturbance}

Current land cover was characterised using ESA WorldCover 2021
\citep{Zanaga2022}, downloaded via the \texttt{geodata} R~package.
Eleven class-specific fractional cover layers (trees, grassland,
shrubland, cropland, built-up, bare, snow/ice, water, wetland,
mangroves, moss/lichen) were aggregated from native ${\sim}$10~m to
0.1\textdegree{} by mean. Six of these fractions (trees, grassland,
shrubland, cropland, bare, wetland) were included as continuous
predictors.

Disturbance history was captured by two date-aware products. Forest loss
year was extracted from the Hansen Global Forest Change v1.10 dataset
\citep{Hansen2013}; a binary flag indicated whether loss occurred before
each site's sampling date. Fire history was derived from the MODIS
MCD64A1 burned area product \citep[2001--2020;][]{Giglio2018}, with
cumulative burn count before sampling used as a continuous predictor.

\subsubsection{Biological}

Microbial community structure was represented by three independent,
spatially explicit datasets. Global soil fungal proportion (fungal
biomass as a fraction of total microbial biomass) was obtained from
\citet{Yu2022} at ${\sim}$1~km resolution. Root colonisation rates by
arbuscular mycorrhizal (AM) and ectomycorrhizal (EcM) fungi were taken
from the global maps of \citet{Barcelo2023}, along with a derived
EcM:AM root colonisation ratio. Mycorrhizal richness and endemism
(rarity-weighted richness) for both AM and EcM fungi were sourced from
the SPUN Global Underground Atlas \citep{VanNuland2025}, together with
a derived EcM:AM richness ratio. All biological layers were aggregated
to 0.1\textdegree{} by bilinear resampling.

\subsubsection{Spatial alignment}

All raster covariates were resampled to a common 0.1\textdegree{}
($\sim$11~km) global grid in WGS84 (EPSG:4326). Continuous variables
were resampled using bilinear interpolation; categorical variables
(K\"oppen zone, WRB class) used modal aggregation. Point-level
extraction was performed at native resolution where available and from
the 0.1\textdegree{} layers otherwise.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Random Forest modelling}
\label{sec:rf}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

All RF models were fitted using the \texttt{ranger} R~package
\citep{Wright2017} with 500~trees, a minimum node size of~5, and the
default~$m_\text{try}$ ($= \lfloor\sqrt{p}\rfloor$, where~$p$ is the
number of predictors). The response variable was log-transformed transit
time ($\ln\,\tau$); predictions were back-transformed via
exponentiation. Parallel computation used all available cores minus one.

Seven nested model configurations were fitted
(Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison}): M1~(Climate only),
M2~(Climate + Edaphic), M3~(Climate + Land~Use),
M4~(Climate + Biological), M5~(Climate + Edaphic + Land~Use),
M6~(Climate + Edaphic + Biological), and M7~(Full: all four groups).
This design holds the climate baseline constant across all models, so
that the change in performance between M1 and each subsequent model
directly quantifies the additional variance explained by each process
class.

Model accuracy was assessed by spatial block cross-validation to avoid
spatial autocorrelation-driven overfitting. The global domain was divided
into 2\textdegree{}~$\times$~2\textdegree{} grid cells ($\sim$200~km at
the equator); grid cells were randomly assigned to one of ten folds
(stratified by cell, not by individual observation). For each fold, the
model was trained on the remaining nine folds and predictions were
collected for the held-out block. Cross-validated $R^2$, RMSE, and MAE
were computed on the log-scale predictions pooled across all folds.
Out-of-bag (OOB) statistics from a final model fitted on all data served
as an additional reference. Permutation-based variable importance was
extracted from the final M7 model.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Global prediction}
\label{sec:prediction}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Each of the seven trained models was applied to the aligned
0.1\textdegree{} global raster stack to produce a spatially continuous
transit time prediction map. Predictions were generated in log space and
back-transformed to years. Difference maps between successive model
configurations (e.g., M2~$-$~M1, M7~$-$~M1) were computed to visualise
the spatial signature of each process class on predicted $\tau$.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Sensitivity analysis}
\label{sec:ale}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

We used Accumulated Local Effects \citep[ALE;][]{Apley2020} rather than
partial dependence plots to characterise the marginal response of
$\tau$ to each continuous predictor. ALE avoids the extrapolation
artefacts inherent in partial dependence by evaluating prediction
differences only within narrow quantile-based bins of the focal
variable, so that correlated predictors retain their naturally
co-occurring values.

The ALE analysis was conducted on a spatially stratified random
subsample of 100\,000 pixels drawn from the global 0.1\textdegree{}
prediction grid (stratified by 5\textdegree{} latitude--longitude cells
to ensure geographic representativeness). Each continuous predictor was
divided into 40~quantile-based bins. Within each bin, the focal variable
was nudged to the bin's upper and lower edge while all other variables
were held at their observed values; the local prediction difference was
accumulated across bins and centred to zero at the distribution's lower
edge to produce a response curve in native transit time units (years).
Categorical predictors (K\"oppen zone, WRB functional group code) were
excluded from ALE.

To examine latitudinal variation in predictor responses, ALE curves were
additionally computed within four latitude bands: southern extratropics
($<$$-$23.5\textdegree{}), tropics ($-$23.5\textdegree{} to
23.5\textdegree{}), northern temperate (23.5\textdegree{} to
66.5\textdegree{}), and boreal/Arctic ($>$66.5\textdegree{}).

\subsection{Software and reproducibility}
\label{sec:software}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

All analyses were conducted in R~($\geq$~4.3) using the following key
packages: \texttt{terra} \citep{Hijmans2024} for raster and vector
operations, \texttt{sf} for spatial data, \texttt{ranger}
\citep{Wright2017} for Random Forest modelling, \texttt{randomForest}
for bulk density gap-filling, \texttt{geodata} for ESA WorldCover
download, \texttt{ggplot2} for visualisation, and
\texttt{dplyr}/\texttt{tidyr} for data manipulation. ERA5-Land data
were downloaded from the Copernicus Climate Data Store; SoilGrids layers
were accessed via ISRIC's cloud-optimised GeoTIFF service. The analysis
pipeline consists of 18~numbered R~scripts executing sequentially from
data loading through global prediction and sensitivity analysis. Code
and intermediate outputs are archived at [DOI].
