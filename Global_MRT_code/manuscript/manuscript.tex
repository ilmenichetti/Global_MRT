\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% =============================================================================

\paragraph{SOC turnover governs the climate feedback.}

Soils store approximately twice as much carbon as the atmosphere, and the
rate at which this carbon cycles through the soil system is a primary
determinant of the land carbon--climate feedback. Whether soils act as a
net source or sink under changing conditions depends not on stocks alone
but on turnover --- specifically, on the mean transit time ($\tau$) of
carbon through the soil, defined as the average time elapsed between a
carbon atom entering and leaving the system
\citep{Sierra2017,Sierra2018}. Even modest shifts in transit times
across large areas can alter the global carbon balance substantially.

\paragraph{The classical paradigm: chemistry and constants.}

Since the foundational models of the 1970s--1990s
% [CITE: Jenkinson, Parton/CENTURY, RothC lineage]
, soil organic carbon (SOC) decomposition has been represented as a
first-order process: the rate of loss from each pool is proportional to
its mass, scaled by functions of temperature and moisture. The rate
constant~$k$ --- and thus the transit time $\tau = 1/k$ --- is treated as
an intrinsic property of the pool, spatially invariant once the
environmental scalars are applied. This framework descends from the
chemical recalcitrance paradigm, in which substrate chemistry determines
decomposability, and the chemistry of soil organic matter is assumed
broadly similar across environments. Earth system models (ESMs) still
largely rely on this architecture.
% [CITE: Todd-Brown et al. 2013; Wieder et al. 2013]

\paragraph{The paradigm shift: persistence as an ecosystem property.}

Over the past two decades, this view has been increasingly challenged.
\citet{Schmidt2011} argued that SOC persistence is not a molecular
property but an ecosystem property, emerging from interactions between
organic matter, mineral surfaces, microbial communities, and the
physical structure of the soil matrix. Subsequent work has reinforced
this perspective: organo-mineral associations and reactive mineral
surfaces stabilise organic matter independently of its chemistry
% [CITE: Kleber et al. 2007, 2021; Lehmann \& Kleber 2015]
; microbial carbon use efficiency varies with community composition
% [CITE: Kallenbach et al. 2016; Cotrufo et al. MEMS]
; priming effects modulate the decomposition of existing stocks in
response to fresh inputs; and mycorrhizal pathways influence both the
quantity and chemical nature of belowground carbon transfers
% [CITE: Averill et al. 2014]
. The implication is that decomposition kinetics are not constant ---
they arise from a web of interacting ecological, edaphic, and
biological processes operating locally.

\paragraph{The zonality hypothesis.}

If transit times are emergent properties of local ecology, they should
exhibit spatial structure --- a ``zonality'' analogous to the classical
pedogenic zonality where soil types co-vary with climate, parent
material, organisms, topography, and time. Under this view, SOC transit
times would not be universal constants scaled by temperature and
moisture alone, but would carry a residual spatial signal reflecting
edaphic, biological, and land-use context. This residual represents the
variance that current ESMs do not capture.

\paragraph{The modelling dilemma.}

One could attempt to represent these interactions mechanistically, but
doing so introduces a different class of risk. Any mechanistic model
imposes a simplified structure on a complex, potentially nonlinear
system. Processes may be omitted not because data are lacking but
because they are not yet recognised, or because they operate through
interactions invisible at the chosen scale of simplification. The model
then risks encoding our current biases about which processes matter: a
detailed mechanistic representation that is wrong about the structure
of interactions may describe calibration data well (SOC models are
already overparameterised relative to available observations) yet
fail to extrapolate spatially and, more critically, temporally. Working
instead at the level of the emergent kinetic property (the
observable, bulk transit time) and exploring its spatial covariance
with environmental covariates may introduce less structural error when
extrapolating, precisely because it does not commit to a specific
internal process architecture that may be incomplete or wrong.
Introducing the zonality of SOC dynamics directly into models
(parameterising transit times as spatially varying,
reflecting local ecological context) could be a way to capture this
missing variance without the risks of mechanistic overspecification.

\paragraph{This study.}

As an exploratory step in that direction, we compile a global dataset of
soil profiles, calculate apparent mean transit times under steady-state
first-order assumptions \citep[sensu][]{Sierra2017,Sierra2018}, and ask:
how much of the spatial variation in transit times can be attributed to
processes beyond climate, and which process classes matter where? We use
a nested Random Forest framework to partition variance across four
groups (climate, edaphic, land use, and biological) where the
change in performance between the climate-only baseline (M1) and the
full model (M7) directly quantifies the ``missing zonality'' that
current ESMs do not represent. The steady-state assumption is a known
simplification done under the expectations that over a dataset of this
size and geographic scope, errors from non-equilibrium sites are
expected to partially cancel. Accumulated Local Effects analysis
identifies the marginal response of transit times to individual
predictors, revealing the functional shape of the relationships,
information that could inform how zonality might eventually be
parameterised. The resulting observation-constrained maps provide a
benchmark for identifying where and why ESMs might systematically
diverge from data-driven estimates.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}
% =============================================================================

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Conceptual framework}
\label{sec:framework}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

We define the mean transit time ($\tau$) of SOC as the average time a
carbon atom spends in the soil from entry to exit
\citep{Sierra2017,Sierra2018}. Under steady-state assumptions (constant
SOC stock and input flux) and a single homogeneous pool, $\tau$ equals
the ratio of the SOC stock to the annual carbon input flux and is the
inverse of the apparent first-order decomposition rate constant~$k$:

\begin{equation}
  \tau \;=\; \frac{\text{SOC}_\text{stock}}{C_\text{input}}
  \;=\; \frac{1}{k}
  \label{eq:mtt}
\end{equation}

\noindent
We acknowledge that for multi-pool systems, $\tau$ as computed here
represents a bulk diagnostic that conflates pools with different
turnover rates \citep{Sierra2018}. Nevertheless, this metric is widely
used as a first-order characterisation of SOC dynamics and is directly
comparable to the transit time diagnostics computed from ESM output
\citep{Carvalhais2014,Sierra2017}.

To separate the contributions of different process classes to spatial
variation in $\tau$, we designed a nested Random Forest (RF) modelling
framework in which predictor variables are organised into four
mechanistically distinct groups: Climate, Edaphic, Land~Use, and
Biological (Table~\ref{tab:predictors}). Seven model configurations
(M1--M7) progressively combine these groups
(Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison}), so that the change in cross-validated
performance between successive models isolates the additional explanatory
power of each process class while holding the climate baseline constant.

A key design constraint is the exclusion of productivity-related
variables (net primary productivity, leaf area index) from the predictor
set. Because $\tau$ is calculated as SOC stock divided by an NPP-derived
input flux (Eq.~\ref{eq:mtt}), including productivity as a predictor
would introduce a circular dependency.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Soil data compilation}
\label{sec:data}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

We compiled soil profile data from the World Soil Information Service
\citep[WoSIS;][]{Batjes2024} point dataset
(\texttt{sol\_chem.pnts\_horizons}), which integrates observations from
multiple national and regional soil surveys worldwide. From the full
dataset of horizon-level records, we retained only mineral soils by
removing all records with organic carbon (OC)
$\geq$~120~g~kg$^{-1}$ (the conventional threshold separating mineral
from organic soil horizons). Records with missing OC were retained.

Horizon-level data were standardised to a 0--20~cm depth interval by
depth-weighted averaging. For each unique soil profile (identified by
site~ID), we computed the overlap of each recorded horizon with the
0--20~cm target interval and calculated a thickness-weighted mean for
seven soil variables: organic carbon, total nitrogen, pH~(H$_2$O), clay
content, silt content, sand content, and oven-dry bulk density.
Site-level metadata (coordinates, observation date, source database)
were carried forward as the most common value across horizons.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Data quality control}
\label{sec:qc}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

After depth standardisation, the dataset contained 381\,541 profiles from
47~source databases spanning 78\textdegree S to 78\textdegree N. We applied
a series of quality-control filters to remove records likely to introduce
systematic bias into the MRT calculation. First, 83\,206 profiles
(21.8\,\%) reported OC~$=$~0~g\,kg$^{-1}$, which in WoSIS typically
encodes missing data rather than a true measurement; these values were set
to \texttt{NA} and the affected profiles were removed when no valid OC
remained. Second, 102\,379 exact duplicates (matching coordinates and OC)
were removed (26.8\,\% of all records). Third, bulk-density values outside
the physically plausible range ($<$~0.1 or $>$~2.5~g\,cm$^{-3}$) were set
to \texttt{NA}. After filtering, 198\,382 profiles remained (52.0\,\% of
the pre-QC total). Profiles with low-precision (integer-only) coordinates
($n = 208$) and unusually high OC ($>$~100~g\,kg$^{-1}$; $n = 3\,252$)
were flagged but retained.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Bulk density gap-filling}
\label{sec:bd}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Bulk density (BD) is required to convert OC concentration to SOC stock
but is frequently unreported. We gap-filled missing BD values using a
three-tier approach. First, a Random Forest model
(\texttt{randomForest} R~package; 200~trees) was trained on the subset
of profiles with measured BD, using terrain metrics, K\"oppen zone, land
cover fractions, SoilGrids soil properties (excluding BD), MODIS NPP,
WRB functional group, and MODIS burn count as predictors. Ten-fold
cross-validation confirmed that the RF model outperformed the SoilGrids
BD product for sites with measured BD. Second, profiles lacking measured
BD but with complete RF predictors received RF-predicted values. Third,
remaining gaps were filled with SoilGrids BD as a fallback. Each profile
was flagged with its BD source (measured, RF, or SoilGrids).

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Mean transit time calculation}
\label{sec:mtt_calc}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

SOC stock for the 0--20~cm layer was calculated as:

\begin{equation}
  \text{SOC}_\text{stock}\;[\text{g\,C\,m}^{-2}]
  \;=\; \text{OC} \;\times\; \text{BD} \;\times\; 20 \;\times\; 10
  \label{eq:soc_stock}
\end{equation}

\noindent
where OC is in g~kg$^{-1}$, BD in g~cm$^{-3}$, depth is 20~cm, and the
factor~10 converts units.

Carbon input to soil was estimated as the belowground fraction of net
primary productivity (BNPP). We used MODIS MOD17A3 annual NPP
\citep[2001--2020;][]{Running2015} extracted at each profile location,
applying the MODIS scale factor
($\text{NPP}_\text{true} = \text{NPP}_\text{raw} / 10$;
g~C~m$^{-2}$~yr$^{-1}$). To maintain temporal consistency with the
equilibrium assumption, only NPP values from years preceding each
site's sampling date were used. Sites sampled before the MODIS era
(pre-2001) or with fewer than three pre-sampling NPP years were
excluded from the temporal statistics.

Belowground allocation fractions (BNPP/NPP) were assigned by dominant
land cover class following \citet{Mokany2006}, \citet{Malhi2011}, and
\citet{Litton2008}: tree cover 0.35, shrubland 0.45, grassland 0.55,
cropland 0.30, herbaceous wetland 0.50, mangroves 0.40, moss/lichen
0.60. Sites lacking land cover information received a global average of
0.40. Soil carbon input was then:

\begin{equation}
  C_\text{input}\;[\text{g\,C\,m}^{-2}\text{\,yr}^{-1}]
  \;=\; \text{NPP} \;\times\; f_\text{BNPP}
  \label{eq:c_input}
\end{equation}

\noindent
and $\tau$ was computed as the ratio of SOC stock to $C_\text{input}$
(Eq.~\ref{eq:mtt}). Quality control flags were assigned: values with
$\tau < 1$~year or $> 1000$~years, or with missing, negative, or
infinite values were excluded. The upper and lower 1\% tails of the
remaining distribution were removed prior to model fitting.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Predictor variables}
\label{sec:predictors}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Predictor variables were organised into four groups reflecting distinct
mechanisms influencing SOC turnover (Table~\ref{tab:predictors}).

\subsubsection{Climate}

Climate variables were derived from the ERA5-Land reanalysis monthly
means \citep[2001--2020;][]{MunozSabater2021}. From the original monthly
fields of 2-m air temperature (\texttt{t2m}), soil temperature at
layers~1 (0--7~cm; \texttt{stl1}) and~2 (7--28~cm; \texttt{stl2}),
volumetric soil water at layers~1 (\texttt{swvl1}) and~2
(\texttt{swvl2}), snow cover fraction (\texttt{snowc}), and potential
evaporation (\texttt{pev}), we calculated the following annual
statistics: mean 2-m temperature, temperature seasonality (monthly
standard deviation), depth-weighted mean soil temperature for 0--20~cm
(weights 0.35 for layer~1 and 0.65 for layer~2, proportional to the
overlap with the 0--20~cm interval), depth-weighted mean soil moisture
(same weights), mean snow cover fraction, and mean potential
evaporation. Climate zone was represented by the K\"oppen-Geiger
classification of \citet{Beck2023} at 30-class resolution (numeric
codes 1--30), based on the 1991--2020 climatological period at
${\sim}$1~km native resolution, resampled to 0.1\textdegree{} by modal
aggregation.

\subsubsection{Edaphic}

Soil properties were obtained from SoilGrids 250\,m v2.0
\citep{Poggio2021}, depth-weighted to 0--20~cm: clay content, sand
content, bulk density, coarse fragment volume, pH~(H$_2$O), cation
exchange capacity, and total nitrogen. All values were converted from
SoilGrids integer encoding to standard units using published
\texttt{d\_factor} scale factors (divisor~10 for texture, pH, CEC, and
coarse fragment volume; divisor~100 for bulk density and nitrogen).
Scale conversion was applied consistently to both point-extracted
training data and raster layers used for global prediction and
sensitivity analysis.

Terrain metrics were derived from the Copernicus GLO-30 Digital
Elevation Model \citep{CopernicusDEM2022}, aggregated to
0.1\textdegree{}: mean elevation, mean slope, northness and eastness
(sine and cosine of aspect), and terrain ruggedness (focal standard
deviation of elevation in a $3 \times 3$ cell window, $\sim$30~km).

Soil classification was based on the SoilGrids WRB Most Probable class
\citep[250\,m;][]{Poggio2021}, aggregated to 0.1\textdegree{} by mode
and reclassified into eight mechanistically defined functional groups:
sandy/coarse (Arenosols, Podzols, Regosols), temperate agricultural
(Luvisols, Cambisols, Phaeozems, Chernozems, Kastanozems,
Albeluvisols), high-clay tropical (Acrisols, Ferralsols, Lixisols,
Plinthosols, Nitisols, Alisols, Vertisols), arid/chemical (Calcisols,
Gypsisols, Solonchaks, Solonetz, Durisols), poorly drained (Gleysols,
Histosols, Fluvisols, Stagnosols, Planosols), volcanic (Andosols),
shallow/young (Leptosols, Umbrisols), and cold/permafrost (Cryosols).
These groups were encoded as integer codes for the RF models.

\subsubsection{Land use and disturbance}

Current land cover was characterised using ESA WorldCover 2021
\citep{Zanaga2022}, downloaded via the \texttt{geodata} R~package.
Eleven class-specific fractional cover layers (trees, grassland,
shrubland, cropland, built-up, bare, snow/ice, water, wetland,
mangroves, moss/lichen) were aggregated from native ${\sim}$10~m to
0.1\textdegree{} by mean. Six of these fractions (trees, grassland,
shrubland, cropland, bare, wetland) were included as continuous
predictors.

Disturbance history was captured by two date-aware products. Forest loss
year was extracted from the Hansen Global Forest Change v1.10 dataset
\citep{Hansen2013}; a binary flag indicated whether loss occurred before
each site's sampling date. Fire history was derived from the MODIS
MCD64A1 burned area product \citep[2001--2020;][]{Giglio2018}, with
cumulative burn count before sampling used as a continuous predictor.

\subsubsection{Biological}

Microbial community structure was represented by three independent,
spatially explicit datasets. Global soil fungal proportion (fungal
biomass as a fraction of total microbial biomass) was obtained from
\citet{Yu2022} at ${\sim}$1~km resolution. Root colonisation rates by
arbuscular mycorrhizal (AM) and ectomycorrhizal (EcM) fungi were taken
from the global maps of \citet{Barcelo2023}, along with a derived
EcM:AM root colonisation ratio. Mycorrhizal richness and endemism
(rarity-weighted richness) for both AM and EcM fungi were sourced from
the SPUN Global Underground Atlas \citep{VanNuland2025}, together with
a derived EcM:AM richness ratio. All biological layers were aggregated
to 0.1\textdegree{} by bilinear resampling.

\subsubsection{Spatial alignment}

All raster covariates were resampled to a common 0.1\textdegree{}
($\sim$11~km) global grid in WGS84 (EPSG:4326). Continuous variables
were resampled using bilinear interpolation; categorical variables
(K\"oppen zone, WRB class) used modal aggregation. Point-level
extraction was performed at native resolution where available and from
the 0.1\textdegree{} layers otherwise.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Random Forest modelling}
\label{sec:rf}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

All RF models were fitted using the \texttt{ranger} R~package
\citep{Wright2017} with 500~trees, a minimum node size of~5, and the
default~$m_\text{try}$ ($= \lfloor\sqrt{p}\rfloor$, where~$p$ is the
number of predictors). The response variable was log-transformed transit
time ($\ln\,\tau$); predictions were back-transformed via
exponentiation. Parallel computation used all available cores minus one.

Seven nested model configurations were fitted
(Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison}): M1~(Climate only),
M2~(Climate + Edaphic), M3~(Climate + Land~Use),
M4~(Climate + Biological), M5~(Climate + Edaphic + Land~Use),
M6~(Climate + Edaphic + Biological), and M7~(Full: all four groups).
This design holds the climate baseline constant across all models, so
that the change in performance between M1 and each subsequent model
directly quantifies the additional variance explained by each process
class.

Model accuracy was assessed by spatial block cross-validation to avoid
spatial autocorrelation-driven overfitting. The global domain was divided
into 2\textdegree{}~$\times$~2\textdegree{} grid cells ($\sim$200~km at
the equator); grid cells were randomly assigned to one of ten folds
(stratified by cell, not by individual observation). For each fold, the
model was trained on the remaining nine folds and predictions were
collected for the held-out block. Cross-validated $R^2$, RMSE, and MAE
were computed on the log-scale predictions pooled across all folds.
Out-of-bag (OOB) statistics from a final model fitted on all data served
as an additional reference. Permutation-based variable importance was
extracted from the final M7 model.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Global prediction}
\label{sec:prediction}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Each of the seven trained models was applied to the aligned
0.1\textdegree{} global raster stack to produce a spatially continuous
transit time prediction map. Predictions were generated in log space and
back-transformed to years. Difference maps between successive model
configurations (e.g., M2~$-$~M1, M7~$-$~M1) were computed to visualise
the spatial signature of each process class on predicted $\tau$.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Sensitivity analysis}
\label{sec:ale}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

We used Accumulated Local Effects \citep[ALE;][]{Apley2020} rather than
partial dependence plots to characterise the marginal response of
$\tau$ to each continuous predictor. ALE avoids the extrapolation
artefacts inherent in partial dependence by evaluating prediction
differences only within narrow quantile-based bins of the focal
variable, so that correlated predictors retain their naturally
co-occurring values.

The ALE analysis was conducted on a spatially stratified random
subsample of 100\,000 pixels drawn from the global 0.1\textdegree{}
prediction grid (stratified by 5\textdegree{} latitude--longitude cells
to ensure geographic representativeness). Each continuous predictor was
divided into 40~quantile-based bins. Within each bin, the focal variable
was nudged to the bin's upper and lower edge while all other variables
were held at their observed values; the local prediction difference was
accumulated across bins and centred to zero at the distribution's lower
edge to produce a response curve in native transit time units (years).
Categorical predictors (K\"oppen zone, WRB functional group code) were
excluded from ALE.

To examine latitudinal variation in predictor responses, ALE curves were
additionally computed within four latitude bands: southern extratropics
($<$$-$23.5\textdegree{}), tropics ($-$23.5\textdegree{} to
23.5\textdegree{}), northern temperate (23.5\textdegree{} to
66.5\textdegree{}), and boreal/Arctic ($>$66.5\textdegree{}).

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{ESM benchmarking}
\label{sec:esm}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

To place our observation-constrained $\tau$ estimates in the context of
current Earth system model (ESM) behaviour, we compiled and harmonised
soil carbon turnover diagnostics from six CMIP6 models, each
representing a structurally distinct land surface model (LSM) lineage
following the model-selection strategy of \citet{Varney2022}: CESM2
(CLM5; NCAR), UKESM1-0-LL (JULES; MOHC), IPSL-CM6A-LR (ORCHIDEE;
IPSL), MPI-ESM1-2-LR (JSBACH; MPI-M), CanESM5 (CLASS-CTEM; CCCma),
and MIROC-ES2L (VISIT-e; MIROC).

For each model we extracted monthly \texttt{cSoil} (total soil carbon
stock) and \texttt{rh} (heterotrophic respiration) from the
\texttt{r1i1p1f1} historical ensemble member and computed the
climatological mean over 1980--2014. Effective soil carbon turnover time
was calculated as the quasi-equilibrium ratio
\citep{Koven2017,Carvalhais2014}:

\begin{equation}
  \tau_\text{ESM} \;=\; \frac{\text{cSoil}}{\text{rh}}
  \label{eq:tau_esm}
\end{equation}

\noindent
with \texttt{rh} converted from kg\,C\,m$^{-2}$\,s$^{-1}$ to an annual
flux (kg\,C\,m$^{-2}$\,yr$^{-1}$). Because CESM2 reports \texttt{cSoil}
integrated over the full simulated soil column whereas the remaining
models report stocks of shallower effective depth, we substituted
\texttt{cSoilAbove1m} for CESM2 to improve vertical comparability with
both the other ESMs and our 0--20~cm observation-based estimate. We
acknowledge that residual depth and pool-definition differences persist
across the ensemble; these are unavoidable in cross-ESM $\tau$
comparisons and are the reason we emphasise relative spatial structure
rather than absolute values in our analysis
\citep[see also][]{Varney2022}.

All model outputs were bilinearly regridded onto a common
1\textdegree{} global grid (WGS84). Pixels with
$\tau_\text{ESM} < 1$~yr or $> 10\,000$~yr were masked as physically
implausible. We then computed the six-member ensemble mean and the
across-model standard deviation as a measure of structural disagreement
among LSMs. For comparison with the RF~M7 prediction, we reprojected
the RF map to the same 1\textdegree{} grid.

To compare spatial structure on a common basis across products with
different absolute magnitudes, we computed area-weighted zonal means of
$\tau$ in 1\textdegree{} latitude bands for each product and applied two
normalisations. In the main text (Fig.~\ref{fig:esm_heatmap}) we apply a
per-product min--max normalisation,
$\tau_\text{norm} = (\tau - \tau_\text{min})/(\tau_\text{max} -
\tau_\text{min})$, which rescales every product to the interval
$[0, 1]$ and thereby exposes the \emph{shape} of each product's
latitudinal distribution independent of its absolute range. In the
appendix (Fig.~\ref{fig:esm_profile}) we show the un-normalised zonal
profiles on a logarithmic axis to preserve magnitude information. To
complement these visualisations with magnitude-free quantitative
measures of spatial dispersion, we additionally computed for each
product the coefficient of variation across land pixels, the
95th-to-5th percentile ratio, and the standard deviation of
$\ln \tau$ (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:esm_dispersion}).

\subsection{Software and reproducibility}
\label{sec:software}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

All analyses were conducted in R~($\geq$~4.3) using the following key
packages: \texttt{terra} \citep{Hijmans2024} for raster and vector
operations, \texttt{sf} for spatial data, \texttt{ranger}
\citep{Wright2017} for Random Forest modelling, \texttt{randomForest}
for bulk density gap-filling, \texttt{geodata} for ESA WorldCover
download, \texttt{ggplot2} for visualisation, and
\texttt{dplyr}/\texttt{tidyr} for data manipulation. ERA5-Land data
were downloaded from the Copernicus Climate Data Store; SoilGrids layers
were accessed via ISRIC's cloud-optimised GeoTIFF service. The analysis
pipeline consists of 20~numbered R~scripts executing sequentially from
data loading through global prediction, sensitivity analysis, and ESM
benchmarking. Code and intermediate outputs are archived at [DOI].

\section{Results}
% =============================================================================

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Data compilation and quality control}
\label{sec:results_data}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The initial WoSIS extraction yielded 1\,459\,728 mineral-soil horizon
records, which depth standardisation collapsed to 381\,541 unique profiles
(0--20~cm). Quality-control filtering (Sect.~\ref{sec:qc}) reduced the
dataset to 198\,382 profiles drawn from 47~source databases, with
GLanCE.points contributing the largest share (21.1\,\%), followed by
USDA\_NCSS (14.2\,\%), LandPKS (8.4\,\%), SISLAC (7.7\,\%), and
Netherlands.BIS (7.4\,\%). Observation dates ranged from 1900 to~2024,
with the majority (79\,\%) falling between~1980 and~2020. Of the
198\,382 profiles, 38\,292 (19.3\,\%) had measured bulk density, 133\,557
(67.3\,\%) received RF-predicted values, and 21\,830 (11.0\,\%) were
gap-filled with SoilGrids. The steady-state MRT calculation
(Sect.~\ref{sec:mtt_calc}) produced valid estimates for 185\,601 profiles
(93.6\,\%), with the remainder flagged as infinite ($n = 719$), missing
input data ($n = 9\,486$), or outside the plausible range
($<$~1~or~$>$~1\,000~yr; $n = 2\,576$). For model fitting, we removed
the 1st and 99th MRT percentiles, retaining 181\,887 profiles
(MRT range 1.9--143.9~yr). Spatial coverage spanned
78\textdegree S--78\textdegree N and 172\textdegree W--176\textdegree E
(Fig.~\ref{fig:sampling}).

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Model performance}
\label{sec:results_models}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The climate-only baseline (M1; 6~predictors) explained 28.0\,\% of the
spatially cross-validated variance in log-transformed MRT
($R^2_\text{CV} = 0.280$, $\text{RMSE}_\text{CV} = 0.696$;
Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison}). Adding edaphic variables (M2)
yielded the largest single-group improvement
($\Delta R^2 = +0.076$), followed by biological variables (M4;
$\Delta R^2 = +0.057$) and land-use variables (M3;
$\Delta R^2 = +0.030$). Combining edaphic and biological variables
with climate (M6; 28~predictors) produced the highest cross-validated
performance ($R^2_\text{CV} = 0.379$, $\text{RMSE}_\text{CV} = 0.648$),
with no additional gain from adding land-use variables in the full model
(M7; $R^2_\text{CV} = 0.379$). The difference between M6 and M7
indicates that the information carried by land-use covariates is
largely redundant once edaphic and biological context is included.
Across all models, out-of-bag $R^2$ was substantially higher than
cross-validated $R^2$ (e.g.\ M7: $R^2_\text{OOB} = 0.596$ vs.\
$R^2_\text{CV} = 0.379$), reflecting the expected penalty of
spatially blocked validation relative to random hold-out.

Note that sample sizes vary across models because each model requires
complete cases for its predictor set: M1 and M3 use the near-complete
climate and land-cover layers ($n \approx 178\,000$), whereas M4, M6,
and M7 require microbial covariates that restrict the sample to
$n \approx 101\,000$--$110\,000$.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Global distribution of predicted MRT}
\label{sec:results_map}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The full-model (M7) prediction, generated at 0.1\textdegree{} resolution,
covers 631\,252 land pixels and yields a global mean MRT of 22.3~yr
(median 21.4~yr; Fig.~\ref{fig:global_mrt}). Predicted values span
3.7--67.0~yr across the global domain. A clear latitudinal gradient
emerged, consistent with temperature control on decomposition kinetics:
the shortest MRT values (below 15~yr) are concentrated in tropical
lowland forests and intensively managed croplands, while the longest
(above 35~yr) occur in boreal forests, montane terrain, and cold
continental interiors. Within each latitudinal band, substantial
regional structure persists --- notably the Pampas--Patagonia
transition, the Tibetan Plateau, the Sahel, and the central-Asian
drylands --- indicating that climate alone does not determine turnover
kinetics and that non-climatic processes modulate the climate baseline
in spatially organised ways, a pattern quantified by the nested model
structure below.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Variable importance and process contribution}
\label{sec:results_importance}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Permutation importance in the full model (M7; Fig.~\ref{fig:importance})
was dominated by three climate variables: temperature seasonality
(0.361), mean snow cover (0.272), and soil temperature (0.179). The
highest-ranked non-climate variables were soil pH (0.156, Edaphic),
AM fungal endemism (0.134, Biological), terrain elevation (0.130,
Edaphic), and sand content (0.128, Edaphic). Aggregated by process
group, Edaphic variables contributed the largest total importance
(1.24 across 13 variables, mean 0.095 per variable), followed by
Climate (1.08 across 6 variables, mean 0.180), Biological (0.69
across 9 variables, mean 0.076), and Land Use (0.41 across 8
variables, mean 0.051). Climate thus contributed the highest
importance per predictor, while Edaphic variables dominated in
aggregate owing to their larger number.

Spatially, the per-pixel magnitude of prediction adjustment
($|M_i - M_1|$) introduced by each non-climate process group
(Fig.~\ref{fig:process_contribution}) provides a complementary view.
Mean absolute adjustments were 4.6~yr for Edaphic, 7.6~yr for Land Use,
and 5.1~yr for Biological, with maxima reaching 69~yr (Edaphic),
93~yr (Land Use), and 75~yr (Biological). The two metrics --- permutation
importance and per-pixel adjustment magnitude --- give consistent but
not identical pictures: Edaphic modulation is spatially pervasive and
moderate in magnitude, clustering in regions with strong soil-property
gradients (mountainous terrain, the Mediterranean basin, Central Asian
drylands, the Pampas--Patagonia transition); Land-Use modulation
produces the largest per-pixel shifts but concentrates at
categorical land-cover boundaries (agricultural belts, deforestation
fronts, high-latitude fire/forest-disturbance mosaics); Biological
modulation is subtler in magnitude but geographically organised,
emerging most strongly in boreal and temperate ecosystems where
mycorrhizal community composition varies sharply. This distinction is
itself informative: Edaphic variables refine $\tau$ through
spatially pervasive but locally modest modulation, whereas Land Use
produces fewer but larger categorical offsets. Consistent with this,
Land Use contributes relatively little unique variance (M6 vs.\ M7
$\Delta R^2 \approx 0$) once Edaphic and Biological context are
included, despite producing the largest local prediction shifts ---
indicating that much of the Land-Use signal is redundant with
Edaphic and Climate information when assessed at the
variance-explained level.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Marginal effects on MRT}
\label{sec:results_ale}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Accumulated Local Effects (ALE) analysis quantified the marginal
response of MRT to each continuous predictor across its observed range
(Fig.~\ref{fig:ale_top10}). The three largest ALE effect ranges again
belonged to climate variables --- temperature seasonality (10.0~yr),
snow cover (8.0~yr), and soil temperature (4.9~yr). Among non-climate
predictors, four edaphic variables entered the top ten: sand content
(4.8~yr), terrain elevation (2.7~yr), soil pH (2.4~yr), and bulk
density (2.2~yr). Fungal proportion (2.3~yr) and AM endemism (1.8~yr)
were the leading biological responses. Potential evaporation (1.8~yr)
closed the top ten.

Several ecologically interpretable nonlinearities emerged. Temperature
seasonality displayed a saturating positive response, with MRT
rising sharply between 1 and 6~K and plateauing above $\sim$10~K. Snow
cover showed a step-like increase near $\sim$50\,\% mean annual cover,
consistent with a cryoprotection threshold. Soil temperature
exhibited a U-shaped response: MRT declined from cold to mesic
conditions (0--20\textdegree{}C) and rose again at very warm soil
temperatures, likely reflecting moisture limitation in warm arid
environments. Sand content displayed a strongly monotonic negative
response: a shift from clay-rich (20\,\%) to sand-rich (80\,\%) soils
reduced predicted MRT by nearly 5 years, consistent with the loss of
mineral-surface stabilisation capacity in coarse-textured soils. Bulk
density showed a threshold response: low-density soils
($<$~1~g\,cm$^{-3}$) had substantially longer MRT, declining sharply
above this threshold and plateauing in typical mineral soil densities
(1.1--1.6~g\,cm$^{-3}$). Soil pH exhibited a rising inverted-U: MRT
increased from acidic soils (pH 4--5) to a peak near pH 7.5--8,
declining at more alkaline values. Fungal proportion had a distinct
threshold: MRT was near-neutral below a fungal biomass fraction of
$\sim$0.2, then dropped sharply by $\sim$2~yr above that threshold,
indicating that fungal-dominated decomposer communities are associated
with substantially faster bulk SOC turnover. AM endemism showed an
inverted-U, with longest MRT at intermediate endemism values.

Latitude-band stratification (Fig.~\ref{fig:ale_latband}) revealed that
the ALE response to temperature seasonality is strongest in the
Boreal/Arctic zone, while in the Tropics the same variable has a
near-zero marginal effect --- a zonal pattern also visible in the soil
pH response, which has its steepest rise in boreal and northern
temperate bands. Biological responses (AM endemism, fungal proportion)
are comparatively consistent across latitude bands, consistent with
mycorrhizal community composition exerting a pervasive influence on
SOC cycling that operates largely independently of the macroclimatic
gradient.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{ESM benchmarking}
\label{sec:results_esm}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Comparison of our observation-constrained $\tau$ map with the six-member
CMIP6 ensemble reveals substantial divergence in both absolute magnitude
and the spatial organisation of turnover time.

In absolute terms, the ESMs predict a much wider dynamic range of
zonal-mean $\tau$ than RF~M7 (Appendix Fig.~\ref{fig:esm_profile}).
The CMIP6 ensemble mean spans roughly 5--10~yr in tropical latitudes to
several hundred years at high northern latitudes, with individual
models (notably CESM2, IPSL-CM6A-LR, MPI-ESM1-2-LR) exceeding
1000~yr in polar bands --- a 50--100-fold latitudinal range. RF~M7
zonal means, by contrast, vary only between approximately 15 and
50~yr across the same latitudinal domain, a 3--5-fold range. The
pixel-level dispersion statistics
(Appendix Table~\ref{tab:esm_dispersion}) confirm that ESMs have
larger absolute spatial variance in $\tau$ than our observation-constrained
estimate, but that this variance is highly skewed toward a polar tail.

When each product is rescaled to its own range to isolate spatial
structure from absolute magnitude (Fig.~\ref{fig:esm_heatmap}), a
qualitatively different picture emerges. The CMIP6 ensemble mean and
four of the six individual LSMs (CESM2, UKESM1-0-LL, IPSL-CM6A-LR,
MPI-ESM1-2-LR) place the overwhelming majority of their latitudinal
domain --- from the tropics through the mid-latitudes and into most of
the boreal zone --- in the lower portion of their own normalised range,
with the upper end of the scale confined to a narrow band of pixels at
high northern latitudes. Only CanESM5 (CLASS-CTEM) and MIROC-ES2L
(VISIT-e) show a gradual and continuously distributed latitudinal
gradient. MPI-ESM1-2-LR additionally produces a localised arid
subtropical $\tau$ maximum near 20--30\textdegree{}~N, not reproduced
by any other model. RF~M7, in contrast, expresses structured variation
distributed across all latitudes, with identifiable signals in the
subtropics, northern temperate zone, boreal zone, and southern mid-
to high-latitudes --- features that are compressed or absent in the
ESM columns.

Taken together, these results indicate that ESMs and the
observation-constrained estimate disagree not only on the magnitude of
$\tau$ but, more diagnostically, on how its spatial variation is
\emph{organised}: ESMs concentrate variation in a high-latitude
extremum, whereas the observation-constrained estimate distributes it
across biomes.

\section{Discussion}
% =============================================================================

\paragraph{Climate dominates, but not alone.}
The nested analysis confirms climate as the single most important
control on SOC transit times globally: temperature seasonality, snow
cover, and soil temperature occupy the top three slots by both
permutation importance and ALE effect range. Yet climate explains only
28\,\% of the spatially cross-validated variance in $\ln \tau$, leaving
a further 10~percentage points captured by non-climatic process
classes. The residual is not statistical noise; it has a geographically
organised structure identified by the ALE analysis and visible in the
spatial pattern of non-climate prediction adjustments
(Fig.~\ref{fig:process_contribution}).

\paragraph{Edaphic context is the largest non-climate signal.}
Adding edaphic variables produces the single largest $\Delta R^2$ and
the largest aggregate permutation importance of any non-climate group.
Sand content, soil pH, bulk density, and CEC all appear in the top 10
ALE responses, each with an interpretable ecological mechanism.
Sand-rich soils turn over faster because coarse texture limits
organo-mineral stabilisation; low bulk density is associated with
longer $\tau$, consistent with porous, structured, aggregated soils
protecting carbon from decomposers; CEC rises with $\tau$, reflecting
the role of exchangeable cation saturation in stabilising organic
matter on mineral surfaces.

\paragraph{pH as a microbial--edaphic bridge.}
The pH response peaking near 7.5--8 and declining at extremes is
noteworthy because pH is conventionally considered an edaphic variable
but in practice acts as a strong control on microbial community
composition, particularly the fungi-to-bacteria biomass ratio. Our
permutation-importance ranking places pH at position~4 globally, and
in the latitude-band ALE it is most responsive in northern temperate
and boreal soils --- zones where acidic, fungi-dominated forest soils
co-occur with the short MRT values our model predicts there. This is
consistent with an alternative interpretation in which pH, fungal
proportion, and MRT form a single coupled axis of variation:
lower-pH ecosystems sustain higher fungal dominance and, at the bulk
soil scale, faster turnover. The inverse relationship between fungal
proportion and MRT visible in Fig.~\ref{fig:ale_top10} reinforces this
reading. A parallel mineral-phase interpretation is also plausible ---
near-neutral to mildly alkaline soils often coincide with
carbonate-rich parent material and calcium-bridging stabilisation ---
but the microbial route is the more parsimonious explanation given the
independent fungal signal.

\paragraph{Biological context through mycorrhizal structure.}
Biological variables contribute the second largest $\Delta R^2$
despite the group having only 9 predictors, indicating high information
per variable. AM endemism and AM richness are the strongest
contributors; root colonisation rates (AM and EcM) are not in the top
ranks despite their mechanistic prominence in the literature.
This suggests that the biogeographic \emph{composition} and
diversity-structure of mycorrhizal communities --- rather than the
quantity of root colonisation --- modulate bulk SOC turnover, in line
with recent work emphasising fungal community diversity over standing
stock.

\paragraph{Land use: large local shifts, redundant information.}
The M6 vs.~M7 comparison shows essentially no additional variance
explained by Land Use once Edaphic and Biological context are
included ($\Delta R^2 < 0.001$). Yet at the pixel level, Land Use
produces the largest magnitude of prediction adjustment
(mean~7.6~yr). These two findings are not contradictory but
complementary: Land Use variables shift $\tau$ substantially in
specific places (cropland mosaics, deforestation fronts, high-latitude
burn scars), but that shift is largely predictable from soil-property
and microbial-community signatures that accompany land-use
transitions. Interpretively, this opens a causal ambiguity that the
observational design cannot resolve: does land use \emph{cause}
edaphic and biological change (agricultural conversion alters pH,
texture, mycorrhizal communities), do both respond jointly to shared
drivers (climate, topography), or do both operate independently and
simply co-vary? At the 0.1\textdegree{} resolution and with
contemporary snapshot covariates, the three readings are
observationally equivalent. What we can say is that, for the purpose
of predicting $\tau$, land use adds little that is not already carried
by other variables.

\paragraph{Process contribution is geographically organised.}
The per-pixel prediction-adjustment map
(Fig.~\ref{fig:process_contribution}) shows that non-climate
contributions are not scattered randomly but organised into
recognisable regional patterns: edaphic modulation clusters in
mountainous and arid-gradient regions, land-use modulation in
agricultural and disturbance mosaics, biological modulation in the
boreal zone and parts of the southern temperate belt. This is the
spatial signature anticipated by the zonality hypothesis: the residual
of climate-only prediction is geographically structured, and different
process classes express themselves preferentially in different biomes.

\paragraph{SOC zonality as an underutilised framework.}
Across all these results, a common thread emerges: the spatial
structure of SOC turnover time is an emergent property of local
ecological context, not a universal kinetic constant modulated by
climate scalars alone. We suggest that treating SOC decay
\emph{zonality} as a working conceptual framework --- analogous to the
pedogenic zonality that has long organised soil classification --- may
help unify the mechanistic findings of the past two decades into a
more predictive synthesis. Unlike a single corrective multiplier on
climate-driven $\tau$, a zonal framework would represent different
regions as having distinct combinations of climate, edaphic,
biological, and land-use modulation, each with its own functional
shape. The fact that each process class peaks in a different
biogeographic zone implies that such a framework cannot be collapsed
into a global average; it must be spatially explicit.

\paragraph{ALE and the shape of zonality.}
Permutation importance tells us \emph{whether} a variable matters,
but not how. ALE analysis provides the functional form of each
predictor's marginal effect on $\tau$, which is the information a
future parameterisation would need. The specific nonlinearities we
identify --- threshold effects (snow cover, bulk density, fungal
proportion), saturating positive responses (temperature seasonality),
monotonic declines (sand), and rising inverted-U shapes (pH, AM
endemism) --- each correspond to recognisable mechanistic classes:
phase transitions, rate-limiting saturation, single-process dominance,
and two-competing-process balances. These shapes are directly
translatable into mathematical functional forms, and provide a concrete
target for how zonality might eventually be parameterised in
mechanistic SOC models.

\paragraph{Zonality of zonality: latitude-band stratification.}
A subtler but equally important finding is that the functional shapes
themselves vary across biomes. The temperature-seasonality response is
steep in the boreal zone and flat in the tropics; soil pH has its
strongest marginal effect in temperate and boreal soils; biological
responses are more uniform across latitudes. This means that a single
global ALE curve per predictor would still underspecify the zonality
we identify: parameterisations informed by these results would need to
be latitude- or biome-dependent, not merely spatially varying in
predictor values.

\paragraph{ESM benchmarking and the missing mid-latitude structure.}
The comparison with the CMIP6 ensemble highlights a structural
difference consistent with the zonality framework. Current ESMs
express nearly all of their spatial $\tau$ variation as a polar
extremum set against a broadly uniform lower-latitude background,
whereas the observation-constrained estimate shows structured
variation distributed across climate zones. ESMs appear to capture
the first-order temperature sensitivity of decomposition but miss the
mid-latitude and sub-tropical structure our ALE analysis attributes to
edaphic, biological, and land-use controls. We note this as a
diagnostic observation rather than a critique: the zonality framework
may offer a useful target against which to evaluate and improve
soil-carbon representations, with the specific functional shapes
identified here as one possible entry point.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Several caveats apply. The steady-state assumption underlying our
$\tau$ calculation is a simplification, not strictly true for
individual sites; we rely on the assumption that non-equilibrium
errors partially cancel across the $\sim$180\,000-profile dataset and
that the resulting bulk diagnostic remains directly comparable to ESM
$\tau$ output. The single-pool formulation conflates pools with
different turnover rates \citep{Sierra2018}. The BNPP allocation
fractions used to estimate carbon input are literature-based constants
that do not capture site-specific root allocation dynamics. Training
data is spatially uneven, with dense sampling in North America and
Europe and sparser coverage in tropical Africa and high-latitude Asia,
so predictions in data-poor regions should be interpreted with
caution. Predictor correlations --- particularly among climate,
edaphic, and land-use variables --- are an inherent feature of the
observational design and cannot be fully disentangled without
experimental or temporal replication. Finally, the CMIP6 comparison
is complicated by residual depth and pool-definition differences
between the 0--20\,cm observational estimate and the effective soil
depths represented by different ESMs, which is why we focus on
relative spatial structure rather than absolute magnitude. These
limitations motivate future work focused on time-resolved transit
times, experimental manipulations that could disentangle correlated
drivers, and targeted sampling in the most undersampled regions.
