\noindent\textbf{Summary.}
Soils hold roughly twice as much carbon as the atmosphere, and it is the rate at
which that carbon cycles, rather than the stock alone, that sets the land
carbon--climate feedback. This rate is usually modelled as a global function of
climate, with the unexplained remainder treated as noise. Yet the time carbon
persists in soil is an emergent property of the whole soil system (its texture and
mineralogy, its decomposer biology, its history of use), and soils themselves are
organised zonally, their forming factors varying in coherent geographic patterns.
Whether the part of turnover that climate does not explain is itself attributable,
organised and mappable has not been established. Here we compile a global set of
mineral-soil profiles, estimate steady-state transit times, and decompose their
variation on held-out geographic regions. Climate explains about a third of the
predictable variance; the rest is attributed to edaphic context, soil biology and
land use, and it is not random but spatially organised, edaphic-led with soil
biology a distinct second axis. Mapped, it recovers a coherent zonal pattern of
turnover, so a data-intensive analysis reconverges on the long-standing pedological
insight that soils, and their emergent properties, are zonal. Because it rests on
observations, this pattern also offers a benchmark that current Earth System Models
render with a poleward gradient several times too steep.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} soil organic carbon; mean transit time; decomposition
kinetics; pedological zonality; Earth system models

Soils hold roughly twice as much carbon as the atmosphere, and the rate at which
that carbon cycles (rather than the stock alone) defines the global carbon balance
and the land carbon--climate feedback. The quantity that governs this balance is
the mean transit time $\tau$, the average time a carbon atom spends in the soil
from input to loss \citep{Sierra2017,Sierra2018}. Because the total stock is so
large, even modest shifts in $\tau$ over large areas move the global balance, so
what controls $\tau$, and how it varies geographically, bears directly on whether
soils keep buffering anthropogenic emissions or begin to amplify them. Constraining
that geographic variation from observations is an active goal, and recent work
exploits the present-day spatial relationship between $\tau$ and temperature as an
emergent constraint on the warming response of soil carbon \citep{Varney2020}, on
the premise that the spatial $\tau$ gradient is predominantly thermal.

That premise follows from a durable simplification. Since the foundational models
of the 1970s--1990s \PH{[cite: Jenkinson; Parton/CENTURY; RothC lineage]},
decomposition has been approximated as first-order, with the rate constant $k$ (and
thus $\tau=1/k$) treated as an intrinsic property of the substrate, spatially
invariant once temperature and moisture scalars are applied; Earth System Models
still largely inherit this architecture \PH{[cite: Todd-Brown 2013; Wieder 2013]}.
The past two decades have recast that view: persistence is now read as an ecosystem
rather than a molecular property, emerging from interactions among organic matter,
mineral surfaces, microbial communities and soil structure
\citep{Schmidt2011}. The implication has not been followed to its spatial
conclusion. If the properties that set persistence arise locally, they should carry
spatial structure, and here pedology offers more than an analogy: soils are
themselves organised zonally, their forming factors varying in coherent geographic
patterns, so an emergent property of the soil system should share part of that
organisation. Read this way the remainder that climate leaves unexplained need not
be noise; it may be organised geography.

We therefore take a route complementary to mechanistic modelling. Rather than
prescribe how non-climate context acts on a system whose processes are only partly
known, we ask what spatial structure the observations already carry once climate is
accounted for. Compiling a global set of mineral-soil profiles and their
steady-state transit times, we ask four questions of the variance beyond climate,
under spatial cross-validation: how much belongs to which process class (climate,
the edaphic matrix, the biological system that supplies and processes carbon, and
land use), attributed order-independently; whether it is organised or merely
scattered; where context lengthens or shortens turnover, mapped at the finest scale
the data can resolve; and how each driver acts. We find that the beyond-climate
signal, though modest in size, is coherently organised and ecologically
interpretable, that mapping it recovers a zonal pattern of turnover, and that this
observation-based pattern departs sharply from the one Earth System Models carry.

\paragraph{Climate leads, but explains only a third.}
On the set of profiles complete across all four predictor groups
($n=150{,}863$), a Random Forest of $\ln\tau$ reached a spatially cross-validated
$R^2=0.39$, against $0.32$ for climate alone (Methods): a beyond-climate increment
of about seven percentage points. Attributing the explained variance
order-independently among the four groups (Shapley decomposition; Methods,
Fig.~\ref{fig:shapley}) places climate at 35\,\%, the edaphic matrix at 33\,\%,
soil biology at 22\,\% and land use at 10\,\%. Climate is thus the single largest
control (temperature seasonality, snow cover and soil temperature lead every
importance ranking), yet it accounts for only about a third of the variance the
model can attribute; the non-climate classes carry the remaining two thirds. The
single-domain skills tell the same story from the other side: edaphic predictors on
their own explain $\tau$ as well as climate does (both $R^2\approx0.32$), biology
next ($0.23$), land use last ($0.11$). That edaphic context should rival climate is,
under the pedological reading, expected rather than surprising: climate is itself a
primary soil-forming factor, so soil properties partly encode the same zonal climate
signal, and the large climate--edaphic overlap (the single domains sum to $0.98$,
far above the full-model $0.39$) is a direct expression of that coupling, not a
redundancy to explain away.

\paragraph{The remainder is organised, not scattered.}
The variance climate leaves behind is spatially structured. The residual of the
climate-only model, aggregated to $2\textdegree$ blocks, has a Moran's $I$ of $0.25$
($p\ll0.001$; Fig.~\ref{fig:zonality}): neighbouring regions carry similar
beyond-climate residuals far more often than a random arrangement would produce, and
this holds across climate zones rather than in one. The organised part is a
meso-scale feature. Most of the point-to-point variation is short-range and
unstructured (about 56\,\% of the residual variance is present at the shortest
separations), and the agreement between the mapped and the observed residual roughly
doubles from the noisy native pixel ($R^2\approx0.11$) to $\approx0.22$ at
1--2\textdegree{} before plateauing, so we map at $2\textdegree$ (Methods). Mapped
there, the modulation of $\tau$ by non-climate context (the log-ratio of the full to
the climate-only prediction) is itself zonally arranged (Fig.~\ref{fig:zonality}):
edaphic modulation clusters on mountainous and arid-gradient regions, land use on
agricultural and disturbance mosaics, and soil biology across the boreal and
southern-temperate belts. A discrete zonation (each block labelled by its largest
Shapley-weighted modulator; Fig.~\ref{fig:zonality}c) makes the pattern explicit:
different process classes dominate the departure from climate in different biomes.
This is the spatial signature the zonal reading anticipates.

\paragraph{Soil biology is a distinct axis, not a restatement of the soil matrix.}
Although the edaphic and biological modulation fields look broadly similar, the
resemblance is mostly the climate signal they share (each is expressed relative to
climate, so both inherit the same backbone). When biology is instead added on top of
the climate-plus-edaphic model, its increment is essentially uncorrelated with the
abiotic field ($r\approx0$; Methods), so soil biology forms its own spatial axis.
The identity of that axis is suggestive rather than settled: mycorrhizal
richness and endemism lead the biological group (root-colonisation intensity adds
negligible unique skill), which points to the biogeographic composition of
mycorrhizal communities, rather than the sheer quantity of colonisation, as what
relates to bulk turnover. Fungal necromass is now recognised as a major precursor of
stable, mineral-associated organic matter, especially at high latitudes
\PH{[cite: Clemmensen 2013; Averill 2014; Frey 2019]}, and the biogeography of
mycorrhizal association is itself strongly zonal \PH{[cite: Tedersoo 2014;
Steidinger 2019]}, so a zonal biological imprint on $\tau$ is plausible on
independent grounds. We flag it as an interesting possibility to test directly, not
an established result, because part of the apparent signal may still be covariation
with edaphic and climatic gradients we cannot separate here.

\paragraph{Drivers act through interpretable, biome-dependent shapes.}
Tracing the response of $\tau$ to each driver while the others keep their
co-occurring values (Accumulated Local Effects; Methods, Fig.~\ref{fig:ale})
resolves the attribution into mechanism. The response shapes are ecologically
legible: coarse (sandy) texture shortens turnover (weaker organo-mineral
stabilisation), rising pH lengthens it to a peak near 7.5--8, snow cover lengthens
it monotonically, and a higher fungal proportion shortens it. Several shapes are not
monotonic, and (more tellingly) several vary by latitude band rather than following
a single global curve: the temperature-seasonality response is strongest in the
boreal/Arctic zone and near-zero in the tropics, and pH is steepest in northern
soils, whereas the biological responses are comparatively uniform across bands.
These are best read not as ready-made functional forms but as hints at what those
forms might look like, and as evidence that turnover is a set of region-specific
sensitivities rather than a single climate-scaled law.

\paragraph{A gentler gradient than Earth System Models carry.}
Because our $\tau$ field rests on observations rather than prescribed kinetics, it
offers a structural benchmark for models. Compared with a six-member CMIP6 ensemble
\citep{Varney2022}, our map diverges in both magnitude and organisation
(Fig.~\ref{fig:esm}). Absolute turnover is not comparable across products (soil
depth, pool definition and reference period all differ), so we compare only the
normalised spatial structure. There the models place most of their spatial variation
in a high-latitude extreme over a comparatively uniform lower-latitude background,
whereas our estimate spreads structure across biomes; the polar-to-tropical contrast
in relative turnover is gentle in our map ($\sim$2.5$\times$) and steep in the
ensemble (median $\sim$9$\times$, about a factor of 3.7 steeper), and the models
disagree among themselves by an order of magnitude. We read this as an inconsistency
worth investigating rather than a verdict on either picture: both are grounded in
data, but the models reach $\tau$ through globally calibrated process forms that
leave little freedom to express local variance, while ours lets the observed spatial
covariation of $\tau$ with its drivers do the work. One caveat tempers it: our field
is blank where global predictors are missing (including parts of the high
latitudes), so part of the apparent difference in poleward steepness may reflect
that domain mismatch. The comparison also qualifies the space-for-time reading of
\citet{Varney2020}: temperature alone explains little of the point-level variation
in $\tau$ ($R^2=0.06$), and the organised, non-thermal residual we recover is
exactly the structure a $\tau$--temperature curve would absorb into an apparent
thermal sensitivity.

\paragraph{Zonality as a working framework.}
Across these results the spatial structure of $\tau$ behaves as an emergent property
of local ecological context rather than a fixed kinetic constant scaled only by
climate. The beyond-climate signal is modest in absolute predictive terms (about
seven percentage points of variance) yet real, spatially organised, ecological
rather than an artefact of data provenance (only 2.4\,\% of the residual variance
lies between source databases; Methods), and interpretable through biome-dependent
response shapes. Treating the zonality of soil-carbon turnover as a working
framework, analogous to the pedogenic zonality that has organised soil
classification for over a century, draws the mechanistic findings of the past two
decades into a form usable for prediction. The consequence for modelling is concrete
and, importantly, does not require adding mechanistic complexity. Because each
process class is most expressed in a different region, a lighter route is to
stratify the calibration of existing models by biome: keep their first-order
kinetics and architecture intact, but let the turnover rate differ among zonal
classes that combine climatic, edaphic and biological context. Working through
calibration rather than structure, this could recover much of the organised
beyond-climate variance without imposing new mechanistic assumptions that may not
hold, and the biome-dependent response shapes we recover indicate which classes such
a scheme would need to distinguish. Soils are complex systems, and complex systems
are seldom well served by purely reductionist description; letting turnover vary by
zonal class captures that emergence directly rather than rebuilding it part by part.

\section*{Methods}

\paragraph{Transit time.}
We define $\tau$ as the average time a carbon atom spends in soil from entry to exit
\citep{Sierra2017,Sierra2018}. Under steady state (constant SOC stock and input) and
a single homogeneous pool it equals the stock-to-input ratio and the inverse of the
apparent first-order rate constant,
\begin{equation}
  \tau \;=\; \frac{\text{SOC}_\text{stock}}{C_\text{input}} \;=\; \frac{1}{k}.
  \label{eq:mtt}
\end{equation}
For multi-pool systems this is a bulk diagnostic that aggregates pools of differing
rates \citep{Sierra2018}, and it is directly comparable to the transit-time
diagnostics computed from ESM output \citep{Carvalhais2014,Sierra2017}.

\paragraph{Soil data and transit-time estimation.}
Mineral-soil profiles were compiled from the OpenLandMap Open Compendium of Soil
Datasets \citep{OpenLandMapSoilDB2025}, retaining records with organic carbon
$<120$~g\,kg$^{-1}$ and depth-standardising seven properties to 0--20~cm by
thickness-weighted averaging. After quality control (removal of encoded-missing
zeros, exact duplicates, and implausible bulk density) 198{,}382 profiles remained;
bulk density was gap-filled by a Random Forest (outperforming the SoilGrids product
in ten-fold cross-validation) with a SoilGrids fallback. SOC stock was
$\text{OC}\times\text{BD}\times20\times10$ (g\,C\,m$^{-2}$). Carbon input was the
belowground fraction of MODIS MOD17A3 NPP \citep{Running2015}, using only pre-sampling
years for temporal consistency with steady state, with belowground allocation
fractions assigned by dominant land cover \citep{Mokany2006,Malhi2011,Litton2008}.
Root-derived carbon is preferentially stabilised in persistent SOC
\PH{[cite: Rasse 2005; Jackson 2017]}, so the belowground input is a deliberate,
conservative boundary; sensitivity to this choice is examined below. After excluding
$\tau<1$ or $>1000$~yr and trimming the 1st/99th percentiles, 181{,}887 profiles
remained ($\tau$ range 1.9--143.9~yr). Observations on peat in the Global Peatland
Map 2.0 \citep{GreifswaldGPM2022} (both tiers; 6{,}922 profiles, 3.5\,\%) were
excluded from the fit, and peat and open water were masked on all maps.

\paragraph{Predictors and modelling.}
Predictors were organised into four mechanistically distinct groups (Climate,
Edaphic, Land use, Biological; Extended Data Table~1), from ERA5-Land
\citep{MunozSabater2021}, K\"oppen--Geiger classes \citep{Beck2023}, SoilGrids
250\,m v2.0 \citep{Poggio2021}, the Copernicus GLO-30 DEM \citep{CopernicusDEM2022},
ESA WorldCover \citep{Zanaga2022}, Hansen forest change \citep{Hansen2013}, MODIS
burned area \citep{Giglio2018}, and mycorrhizal maps \citep{Yu2022,Barcelo2023,VanNuland2025}.
Productivity variables were excluded by design to avoid circularity with the
NPP-derived input. The biological group uses a six-layer set ($\geq96\,\%$ coverage);
three root-colonisation layers that add negligible unique skill while halving
coverage enter only a sensitivity check. All models were fitted with \texttt{ranger}
\citep{Wright2017} on $\ln\tau$.

\paragraph{Spatially-honest skill and attribution.}
Predictive skill is reported as spatial-block cross-validated $R^2$: the globe was
partitioned into $2\textdegree$ ($\sim$200~km) blocks assigned to ten folds, each
fold predicted from a model trained on the others, so that a held-out region is not
propped up by an almost-coincident training neighbour (the residual stays correlated
only to $\approx16$~km). Enlarging the block to $1$ or $5\textdegree$ lowers absolute
skill but shifts each group's attributed share by at most 2 percentage points. To
attribute the explained variance among the four correlated groups we use a Shapley
decomposition: fitting all $2^4=16$ group subsets on a common row set and averaging
each group's marginal contribution over all orders of entry,
\begin{equation}
  \phi_i = \!\!\sum_{S\subseteq N\setminus\{i\}}
  \frac{|S|!\,(|N|-|S|-1)!}{|N|!}\,[\,v(S\cup\{i\})-v(S)\,],
  \label{eq:shapley}
\end{equation}
where $v(S)$ is the cross-validated $R^2$ of the model on subset $S$; the $\phi_i$
sum to the full-model $R^2$ and are reported as percentage shares.

\paragraph{Organisation, mapping and mechanism.}
Spatial organisation of the beyond-climate residual (out-of-sample $\ln\tau$ minus
the climate-only prediction, aggregated to blocks) was tested with Moran's $I$ using
row-standardised $k=8$ nearest-neighbour weights against 999 permutations, at
$2\textdegree$ and $5\textdegree$. The zonality map is the log-ratio
$\ln[\hat\tau_\text{context}/\hat\tau_\text{climate}]$, computed symmetrically for
the abiotic and biological domains; the discrete zonation labels each block by its
largest Shapley-weighted modulation, or "climate alone" below a small threshold. The
mapping scale ($2\textdegree$) was set by an out-of-sample skill curve (agreement of
mapped with observed residual rising from $\approx0.11$ native to $\approx0.22$ and
plateauing across 1--7\textdegree) and by the residual variogram (nugget/sill
$\approx0.56$, range $\approx16$~km). Marginal responses use ALE \citep{Apley2020}
on the full model (40 quantile bins), computed globally and within four latitude
bands.

\paragraph{ESM comparison and robustness.}
We compared the normalised spatial structure of our $\tau$ against six CMIP6 models
\citep{Varney2022} with $\tau_\text{ESM}=\text{cSoil}/\text{rh}$
\citep{Koven2017,Carvalhais2014} over 1980--2014, treating no product as truth and
reporting magnitude-free dispersion and a polar-to-tropical ratio. Robustness checks
(Supplementary Information) bound the main claims: a provenance control (only 2.4\,\%
of residual variance between source databases), biological parsimony, block-size
invariance, and a carbon-input convention sweep. Because the input is set by a
coefficient on one NPP field, a spatially uniform change only rescales $\tau$ and
leaves every scale-invariant quantity (block-CV $R^2$, Shapley shares, Moran's $I$)
unchanged; re-deriving $\tau$ under total-NPP and generous forest-harvest conventions
moves only the land-use share, and the beyond-climate gap is if anything slightly
larger under harvest.

\paragraph{Software and data.}
Analyses used R ($\geq4.3$) with \texttt{terra}, \texttt{sf}, \texttt{ranger}
\citep{Wright2017} and \texttt{gstat}; the pipeline is a sequence of numbered
scripts and each reported number regenerates from a named script. Code and
intermediate outputs are archived at \PH{[DOI]}. Data sources are listed in the Data
availability statement.
